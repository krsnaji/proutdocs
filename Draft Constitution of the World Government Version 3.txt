PREAMBLE

We, the peoples and states united in the pursuit of a just, sustainable, and harmonious development of humanity, adopt this Constitution as the foundation of the global legal order. It is based on the principles of neohumanism, affirming the highest values of unity, justice, and universal welfare.
Recognizing the inseparable connection between humanity, nature, and all forms of life in the universe, we declare that the fundamental duty of governance is to protect and nurture every living being. We commit to ensuring conditions for comprehensive spiritual, intellectual, and physical growth, safeguarding the environment, and upholding the principles of social and economic justice based on rational resource utilization and ethical leadership.
We proclaim that all living beings possess inherent dignity and are equal before the law and this Constitution. Every culture, people, and region of the world hold equal rights and status, excluding any form of discrimination, exploitation, inequality, or special privileges. Humanity is regarded as a single society, free from narrow geographical, religious, racial, or cultural divisions.
This Constitution upholds the supremacy of cardinal human values, including truth, compassion, justice, moral development, and spiritual progress. It guarantees fundamental rights, including the right to spiritual growth, cultural expression, education, social protection, equal access to resources, and responsibility towards future generations in preserving life, biodiversity, and ecological balance.
In the name of universal justice, moral progress, and global cooperation, we proclaim the supreme legal authority of this Constitution, affirming it as the foundation of a new world order. May it serve as an eternal guide for humanity on its path to prosperity, inner harmony, and the indivisibility of all living beings in the universe.






















SECTION I. FUNDAMENTAL PRINCIPLES

Article 1. The Constitution as the Supreme Law and Universal Legal Code

This Constitution shall be the supreme legal instrument, binding universally upon states, societies, and communities that have voluntarily ratified its provisions. It serves as the foundation for establishing a unified legal order grounded in the principles of social justice, equality, and the protection of the rights of all living beings.
The Constitution establishes the principles governing the legal relations between states, societies, individuals, and the natural environment, affirming the unity of humanity, the indivisibility of the rights and freedoms of all living beings, and the responsibility toward future generations. It shall provide the basis for the global legal equilibrium, ensuring consistency between rights and obligations within the governance framework.
All national laws, regulatory instruments, and international agreements shall be fully compliant with the letter and spirit of this Constitution. In cases of contradiction, this Constitution shall prevail as the universal legal standard.
Any amendments, additions, or reforms to governance systems, legislation, or legal practices may only be executed within the framework of this Constitution. The creation of parallel, contradictory, or subversive legal structures that lead to exploitation, discrimination, or the suppression of the rights of living beings is strictly prohibited.
States, societies, and communities that have ratified this Constitution recognize its supremacy in matters concerning global legal order, social justice, the protection of the rights of living beings, law enforcement, environmental protection, and sustainable development. They are obligated to adhere to its provisions within their jurisdiction.
The Constitution shall be binding on all entities that have ratified it. No individual, organization, governmental body, political movement, or any other structure may take actions that directly contradict its provisions.
Violations of the provisions of this Constitution, attempts to undermine its foundations, or the creation of legal systems leading to exploitation shall be considered threats to global security, justice, and peace. Such actions shall be subject to legal review and sanctions, as prescribed by the justice mechanisms of the World Government.
The Constitution shall retain its immutable character in its fundamental principles; however, its procedural and adaptive mechanisms may evolve in response to societal advancements. Any amendments that infringe upon individual rights, social justice, or the principles of legal regulation are prohibited.
In the event of legal conflicts or uncertainties, the interpretation of this Constitution shall be carried out by the judicial system of the World Government, prioritizing universal legal, humanistic, and socially just norms.
This Constitution proclaims its mission to uphold the dignity of all living beings, establish a global legal balance, harmonize relations between societies and nature, and ensure the flourishing of all forms of life on Earth and beyond.

Article 2. Supremacy of Law

The law is the same for all and holds the highest mandatory authority for all individuals, organizations, governmental bodies, and institutions of the World Government, regardless of their social status, political position, economic influence, nationality, religion, or cultural affiliation.
No state, governing body, private association, corporate structure, or any other organization has the authority to violate the provisions of this Constitution, enact or apply laws that contradict its fundamental principles, undermine individual rights and freedoms, social justice, or legal norms.
Any violation of the provisions of this Constitution, including acts of corruption, abuse of power, restriction of basic rights and freedoms, and the exploitation of humanity and nature, shall be considered a breach of the global legal order and shall be subject to adjudication within the judicial system of the World Government.
No agreements, traditions, customs, religious practices, local laws, or cultural norms may justify the violation of fundamental rights, principles of social justice, and legal protection enshrined in this Constitution.
Any law, regulatory act, administrative order, or judicial decision that contradicts the provisions of the Constitution shall be subject to mandatory review and annulment. The alignment of legislation with the Constitution shall be carried out through the judicial and law enforcement bodies of the World Government.
A global legal monitoring mechanism is established by the World Government to ensure compliance of national laws and administrative acts with the fundamental principles of the Constitution. In case of violations, the member state is required to bring its law enforcement practices into alignment with the Constitution. Failure to do so may result in sanctions, including restrictions on international cooperation, financial measures, and legal proceedings.
No individual may invoke ignorance of the law as an excuse for violating its provisions. The duty to know and comply with the Constitution applies to all without exception.
The supremacy of the law includes the obligation of member states to ensure access to justice, a fair trial, and the protection of the rights of every individual, regardless of their social, economic, or legal status.
In the event of legal disputes or uncertainties, the interpretation of the Constitution shall be carried out exclusively by the judicial system of the World Government, prioritizing the principles of social justice, the protection of the rights of living beings, and environmental protection.
All decisions of the judicial and law enforcement bodies of the World Government, as well as the activities of its law enforcement structures, must be based on the principles of equality before the law, justice, and respect for the dignity of every living being.

Article 3. Status and Powers of the World Government

The World Government is the supreme coordinating body of global regulation, tasked with overseeing, monitoring, and intervening in cases of systemic violations of rights, social justice, or global imbalance. It does not directly govern states, but prevents exploitation and mass violations, ensuring adherence to universal rights and principles.
The World Government conducts supranational monitoring in areas requiring global coordination, including but not limited to:
sustainable development and rational use of natural resources;
protection of the environment and biodiversity;
observance of human rights and the rights of all living beings;
development of science, technology, and education for the benefit of humanity within legal standards;
monitoring of economic processes and prevention of systemic exploitation.
States that have ratified the Constitution retain internal sovereignty within the boundaries that do not contradict the universal principles established by this Constitution. Sovereignty cannot be used to justify violations of rights and freedoms established by the Constitution.
Decisions of the World Government are mandatory in cases of violations of universal rights and principles of social justice. They do not interfere with the economic management of states but are mandatory in aspects related to the prevention of exploitation and discrimination.
The Supreme Board of Sadvipras is the highest body for legal and strategic oversight of the functions of the World Government. It does not intervene in the internal politics of member states, provided their actions are in compliance with the Constitution.
The composition of the Supreme Board of Sadvipras is formed based on principles of moral integrity, intellectual competence, and social responsibility. Members of the Board cannot represent political parties, economic groups, or private organizations. Their role is exclusively focused on the protection of universal interests and the prevention of systemic violations.
The World Government must operate transparently, openly, and in the interests of all humanity. All decisions must be based on the principles of social justice, legal oversight, and sustainable development.
In cases of abuse of power or non-compliance with the Constitution by any organ of the World Government, the Supreme Board of Sadvipras has the authority to initiate investigations, suspend the actions of the relevant organs, and hold individuals accountable within the justice system of the World Government.
The powers of the World Government are strictly limited to the framework established by the Constitution. They cannot be extended to direct control over the development of member states.
The World Government cannot interfere in the internal affairs of member states, except in cases provided by the Constitution, when such intervention is necessary to prevent systemic exploitation, mass violations of rights of living beings, environmental catastrophes, acts of aggression, genocide, or other global threats that jeopardize the common good.

Article 4. Fundamental Human Values

The fundamental human values are the foundation for all laws, administrative decisions, and social institutions. These values are directed toward the development of a harmonious society, where priority is given to the protection of the dignity of every living being, strengthening the principles of social justice, and ensuring the welfare of all.
The core fundamental human values include:
Social Equality: the elimination of discrimination and inequality in access to opportunities for all members of society.
Economic Justice: preventing monopolization, ensuring fair distribution of resources, and preventing exploitation.
Legal and Moral Justice: all laws and administrative decisions must be based on principles of honesty, equality, compassion, responsibility, and respect for the dignity of every individual.
Protection of Nature and All Living Beings: acknowledging the interconnectedness of all forms of life and the human duty to preserve the environment, biodiversity, and natural resources for present and future generations.
Public Oversight of Power: creating effective mechanisms to prevent corruption, abuse of power, and ensure transparency in the activities of all governing bodies.
In the event that any law, tradition, administrative practice, or other norms conflict with the fundamental human values, such norms must be subject to mandatory review, correction, or repeal in the prescribed manner.
Any system of thought must adhere to the principles of social justice, legal development, and humanism. No ideology, religion, national concept, or political theory can be placed above the fundamental human values.
Any violation of the fundamental human values is considered an act that undermines the global legal order. Such actions shall be subject to review within the justice system of the World Government.
States and international organizations must contribute to the formation of a culture focused on developing legal responsibility, social values, and a neohumanist worldview. Education, media, and social institutions must promote the strengthening of ethical standards within society.
The protection and development of the fundamental human values are the shared responsibility of all humanity. Member states must cooperate at the international level to promote these values, exchange best practices, and support initiatives aimed at strengthening justice, peace, and sustainable development.
The fundamental human values are not limited to the current generation but include responsibility to future generations for the preservation of ecological balance, biological diversity, cultural heritage, and the right to a dignified life.

Article 5. Principles of Territorial Organization and Sovereignty

The jurisdiction of the World Government extends to all states and territories that have voluntarily ratified this Constitution. These states recognize the supremacy of the Constitution in matters of global importance, including the protection of the rights of all living beings, environmental security, international justice, and sustainable development.
The internal sovereignty of member states is maintained within the limits that do not contradict the universal principles established by this Constitution. Member states have the right to independently determine their internal policies, legislation, cultural, and social institutions, provided that their actions do not violate fundamental human values and international commitments.
All territories within the jurisdiction of the World Government have equal legal status. Special privileges, discriminatory measures, restrictions, or forms of political and economic domination over individual regions, peoples, or cultural groups are not permitted.
The state borders between member states are conditional and should not hinder the free movement of people, exchange of knowledge, scientific and technical cooperation, cultural interactions, and fair trade. Such restrictions may only be imposed in exceptional cases, when necessary to protect public safety or public health, and must strictly adhere to the principles of proportionality and temporality.
Each state or territory that has adopted this Constitution is obliged to uphold the rule of law, the principles of social justice, and fundamental human values; implement the principles of sustainable development, environmental protection, and harmonious coexistence into their national systems; participate in international initiatives aimed at protecting the rights of all living beings and strengthening global security.
Accession to the World Government is based on a democratic decision by the population of the state or territory, confirmed through a national referendum. Upon ratification of the Constitution, the member state assumes all obligations to comply with its provisions and becomes a full participant in the global system of cooperation.
Withdrawal from the World Government is only possible through a decision by the population of the respective state, after fulfilling all international obligations undertaken under the Constitution. The withdrawal process is governed by international law and is subject to monitoring by international legal bodies and the Supreme Board of Sadvipras to prevent potential violations of human rights and threats to international security.
Sovereignty cannot be used as a justification for violating human rights, destroying the natural environment, conducting acts of aggression, supporting terrorism, or exploiting other peoples.
All territorial disputes between member states are to be resolved exclusively by peaceful means, using the international legal mechanisms provided by the Constitution. Armed conflicts, territorial seizures, and acts of violence are strictly prohibited.
Certain territories of global significance (such as Antarctica, the World Ocean, and outer space) may be under the governance of the World Government for the purpose of ensuring their use in the interests of all humanity, protecting the environment, and fostering scientific cooperation.

Article 6. Unified Legal System

The unified legal system established by this Constitution guarantees justice, the rule of law, and the protection of the dignity of all living beings. The recognition of the unity of humanity necessitates the creation of a legal system in which priority is given to universal values and rights rather than private interests. The system operates under the coordination of the World Government, based on the principles of Neohumanism.
The unified legal system encompasses both international and national legal institutions, which operate in alignment, as ensured by the World Government, to maintain justice and protect the rights of all living beings. All legal structures are subject to uniform oversight by the World Government.
Judicial bodies at all levels must act independently from the executive and legislative branches. The independence of judges, guarantees of their impartiality, and protection from external pressures are the foundation of fair justice, governed by the World Government.
An international legal interaction system is established, which harmonizes national laws with international standards, taking into account the principles of Neohumanism and social justice, under the coordination of the World Government.
Legal norms must be adaptable to respond to changes in society, science, and culture, while maintaining fidelity to the principles of justice and Neohumanism, under strict control by the World Government.
All changes and amendments to laws undergo legal review to ensure their compliance with the universal legal standards established by the Constitution and adherence to the principles of Neohumanism. This review is conducted by independent legal commissions recognized by the World Government.
Legal norms that have become obsolete or contradict modern standards of justice are subject to mandatory review, correction, or annulment within the framework of the World Government.
The Constitution mandates that member states and international organizations promote the development of legal culture, legal education, and respect for the law among all segments of the population. Legal education serves as the foundation for forming conscious citizens capable of actively participating in building a just society.
Ensuring the transparency of judicial processes and the accessibility of justice for all citizens, regardless of their social, economic, or political status. Transparency of justice includes public disclosure of judicial decisions, except in cases where this threatens security or confidentiality within international standards.

Article 7: Goals and Mission of Humanity

The primary goal of humanity is to create a just and harmonious society where every individual and every living being is respected, protected, and has the opportunity for comprehensive development.
Humanity is obligated to live in harmony with nature, recognizing the interdependence of all forms of life. Sustainable development, environmental protection, and rational use of resources are essential elements of the global mission.
The promotion of Neohumanist values and social responsibility forms the foundation for both personal and collective growth. Education and cultural development should foster the awakening of the highest human qualities: compassion, honesty, justice, and responsibility.
Humanity must build models of economic and social development that maintain a balance between the needs of the current generation and future generations, preventing the depletion of natural resources and the degradation of the environment.
The creation of a society free from exploitation, discrimination, and oppression. Every individual has the right to a dignified life, access to education, healthcare, social protection, and opportunities for self-realization.
Humanity must strive to strengthen international cooperation based on mutual respect, solidarity, and peaceful coexistence. Conflict resolution is possible only through dialogue, mutual understanding, and the pursuit of the common good.
Science and technology must serve the benefit of all humanity, contributing to the improvement of quality of life, environmental protection, and the integration of ethical standards in scientific research.
The development of humanity is not limited to material progress. Humanity is responsible for the development of consciousness, the strengthening of Neohumanist values, and the creation of conditions for the harmonious coexistence of all forms of life in the Universe.
The strengthening of a culture of peace and non-violence at all levels of society. The promotion of ideals of tolerance, respect for cultural differences, and peaceful conflict resolution.
The recognition of the unity of humanity as one family, where the care for the well-being of each is the concern of all. A sense of global responsibility must become the foundation for decision-making at all levelsâ€”from local communities to world organizations.

Article 8: Responsibility to Future Generations

Humanity bears collective responsibility for the preservation of the planet, its resources, and cultural heritage for future generations. The decisions made today must consider their long-term impact on the sustainability of ecosystems and society.
Environmental protection is an inherent duty of all states, societies, and individuals. Humanity is obligated to prevent pollution, resource depletion, biodiversity loss, and climate change, ensuring a stable and healthy environment for future generations.
The use of natural resources must be carried out rationally and sustainably, with the aim of minimizing environmental impact and preserving natural wealth for future generations.
Every generation is obligated to protect and pass on cultural, scientific, and philosophical achievements to future generations, ensuring the continuity of knowledge, traditions, and values that contribute to the development of humanity.
Scientific and technological developments must consider the potential risks for future generations, prioritizing research aimed at improving quality of life, preserving health, and developing sustainable technologies.
States and international organizations are required to ensure access to quality education, fostering awareness among the youth about the importance of environmental protection, social responsibility, and global solidarity.
Strengthening international mechanisms to coordinate actions for the protection of the planet and ensuring sustainable development. States-members must exchange best practices and technologies to preserve resources and minimize environmental threats.
In cases of scientific or environmental uncertainty, precautionary measures should be prioritized to prevent potential harm to the environment and the health of future generations.
The Constitution recognizes the right of future generations to a clean environment, access to natural resources, cultural heritage, and opportunities for comprehensive development. These rights cannot be infringed upon by the decisions of current generations.
All political, economic, and social strategies must be developed with long-term perspectives in mind to ensure sustainable development and prosperity for future generations.

Article 9: Principles of Ethical Leadership

Leadership at all levels of governance must be based on the highest moral and ethical principles, including honesty, justice, compassion, responsibility, and service to society. Leaders must act in the interest of the common good, rather than personal or narrow group interests.
Leaders are required to demonstrate a high level of personal ethics, respect the rights and dignity of every living being, promote social equality, justice, and foster trust within society.
Each leader bears personal and legal responsibility for their decisions and actions, especially if they lead to violations of human rights, environmental degradation, or other consequences that harm society and the environment.
Leaders must act openly and transparently, providing the public with access to information about their activities. Accountability includes regular reports to citizens and independent oversight bodies.
Any abuse of power, use of office for personal gain, corruption, or attempts to undermine democratic institutions are considered serious violations and shall be strictly punished according to the law.
States and international organizations must ensure the implementation of leadership training programs based on principles of ethical governance, social responsibility, and neohumanism.
International standards of ethical leadership are developed, including mechanisms for assessing the competence and moral responsibility of leaders at both global and national levels.
Leaders must not hold positions or make decisions in situations where there is a conflict of interest that could affect their objectivity. All potential conflicts of interest must be disclosed and addressed in the prescribed manner.
Independent ethical oversight bodies are established to ensure adherence to principles of ethical leadership, investigate violations, and take corrective actions to address unethical behavior.
Ethical leadership must be encouraged at all levels of society. Government and international institutions must create conditions in which honesty, justice, and service to society become the primary criteria for recognizing and promoting leaders.

Article 10: Equality of Cultures and Languages

All cultures, languages, traditions, and forms of self-expression are of equal value and deserve respect. Cultural diversity is the wealth of humanity and the foundation of global harmony and mutual understanding.
Discrimination based on cultural, linguistic, ethnic, religious, or traditional grounds is strictly prohibited. States and international organizations must take measures to prevent cultural discrimination and oppression.
All languages have the right to development, preservation, and use. States members are obliged to support the protection and promotion of languages, especially those that are at risk of extinction.
Every person has the right to freely practice, develop, and transmit their culture, language, religious beliefs, and traditions, provided that this does not infringe upon the rights and freedoms of others.
Special attention is given to the protection of the rights of indigenous peoples and national minorities. States must respect their cultural identity, traditional knowledge, and right to autonomous development.
Educational systems must reflect the cultural diversity of humanity, promoting respect for different cultures, histories, and traditions, and fostering tolerance, intercultural dialogue, and mutual understanding.
Every person has the right to access cultural goods, art, and cultural heritage, regardless of social, economic, or geographical status.
States members are obliged to cooperate internationally to protect cultural heritage, exchange cultural experiences, and support initiatives aimed at preserving and developing cultural diversity.
In the digital age, states and international organizations must promote the preservation and dissemination of cultural values through modern technologies, ensuring the accessibility of cultural heritage for future generations.
Culture is a living and evolving phenomenon. The Constitution recognizes the right of cultures to evolve and interact while protecting their uniqueness and historical significance.































SECTION II. RIGHTS, FREEDOMS, AND RESPONSIBILITIES

Article 11: Universality and Inalienability of Rights and Freedoms

The rights and freedoms enshrined in this Constitution are universal, inalienable, and belong to every individual from birth. They are based on the principles of social justice, equality, and harmonious coexistence, recognizing the value of all forms of life and the necessity of their protection, irrespective of gender, race, nationality, language, religion, social origin, beliefs, age, or any other status.
These rights and freedoms cannot be restricted or revoked under any circumstances, except in cases explicitly provided for by the Constitution, and strictly limited by the necessity to protect the rights of others, public safety, health, and sustainable development. Any restrictions must comply with the principles of justice, equality, and proportionality.
Rights and freedoms apply to all individuals within the territories of member states, as well as to living beings and natural ecosystems, within the framework of recognizing their right to exist, be protected, and develop harmoniously.
All rights and freedoms are interconnected and indivisible. The exercise of one right must not violate or restrict other rights and freedoms and cannot be used for discrimination or oppression.
Rights and freedoms have direct effect and shall be protected by all government authorities, judicial bodies, international structures, and civil initiatives and organizations acting in accordance with this Constitution. Every individual has the right to their protection in national and international courts.
The rights and freedoms enshrined in this Constitution may evolve and be supplemented in response to advances in science, technology, and changes in societal values, provided they align with the core principles of social justice, equality, and sustainable development.
The exercise of rights and freedoms must not violate the rights and freedoms of others, undermine international legal order, sustainable development, or global well-being.
States are obligated to ensure equal protection of rights and freedoms for all, creating conditions under which every individual can exercise their rights on an equal basis.
The Constitution allows for the development of new rights and freedoms, including those addressing new needs of humanity, in response to changes in society, science, and culture.
Rights and freedoms established by international law and treaties are an integral part of the legal system and cannot be restricted by national legislation if such restriction leads to a reduction in the level of their protection.
Rights and freedoms extend not only to the present generations but also to future generations, with consideration for the responsibility to preserve the environment, cultural heritage, and sustainable development for future inhabitants of the planet.

Article 12: Equality and Non-Discrimination

All people are born free and equal in dignity and rights. Equality is based on the principles of social justice, respect for the dignity of each person, and harmonious coexistence. This principle serves as the foundation for justice, social harmony, and legal protection for every living being.
Discrimination based on race, skin color, gender, language, religion, political or other beliefs, national, ethnic or social origin, property status, birth, disability, age, sexual orientation, gender identity, ecological lifestyle, or any other characteristic that violates the principles of social justice and equality is prohibited.
States and international organizations are obligated to take all necessary measures, including legislative, administrative, educational, and cultural, to eliminate all forms of discrimination. These measures should promote not only formal but also substantive equality, strengthening the values of social justice, sustainable development, and equal opportunities for all.
The Constitution guarantees equal rights and opportunities for all individuals, regardless of gender. States are required to promote the elimination of gender inequality and ensure equal participation of women and men in all spheres of public, political, and economic life.
Special attention shall be given to the protection of the rights of vulnerable groups, including children, the elderly, persons with disabilities, refugees, migrants, and those who face systemic discrimination.
All cultures, languages, religions, and forms of identity are equal before the law. States are required to protect cultural diversity and create conditions for the free and equal development of all cultural groups.
The Constitution affirms the right to equal access to economic opportunities, including the right to work, fair wages, social protection, and participation in the economic life of society, without discrimination on any grounds.
Every person has the right to equal protection under the law without discrimination. All individuals are equal before the courts and have equal access to justice, regardless of their social, economic, or legal status.
The promotion of racial, ethnic, religious, gender, or any other form of hatred that incites discrimination, hostility, or violence is prohibited and shall be subject to legal prosecution at both the international and national levels.
National and international mechanisms shall be established to monitor the implementation of the principles of equality and non-discrimination, as well as to protect the rights of victims of discrimination and hold perpetrators accountable. These mechanisms must ensure the effective enforcement of these principles and the accountability of offenders.

Article 13: The Right to Life, Liberty, and Personal Security

Every person has the inalienable right to life, which is recognized as the highest value and is protected by law. This right includes protection not only from unlawful deprivation of life but also from threats that endanger life and health. No one shall be arbitrarily deprived of life, except in cases provided by international law, aimed at protecting other lives.
The death penalty is prohibited in all forms and circumstances. States members must abolish it from their legal systems and replace it with humane forms of punishment focused on rehabilitation, restoration of justice, and the protection of human dignity.
Every person has the right to liberty and personal security. No one shall be arbitrarily arrested, detained, or deprived of their liberty, except in cases prescribed by law and established procedures, in full compliance with all international standards of justice.
No one shall be subjected to physical, psychological, or moral violence. States are obligated to take all necessary measures to prevent violence, protect victims, and hold perpetrators accountable.
Arbitrary detention, arrests, torture, forced disappearances, and other forms of violations of personal freedom are considered serious crimes against humanity and shall be subject to international legal prosecution.
Every person has the right to protection from all forms of threats to their life, liberty, and security, including threats related to discrimination, persecution, exploitation, and violence.
States are obligated to provide special protection for children, the elderly, persons with disabilities, victims of violence, and other vulnerable groups at increased risk.
States bear direct responsibility for protecting the right to life and personal security of every individual. They must establish legal, institutional, and social mechanisms to prevent violations and ensure justice.
Every person has the right to defend their life and liberty from imminent threats, provided that the principles of proportionality and legality are respected.
Even in times of war, states of emergency, or other crises, the right to life and liberty cannot be entirely restricted. Any temporary limitations must comply with international standards and be subject to independent international oversight.

Article 14: Personal Integrity and Freedom from Torture

Every person has the right to personal integrity, including protection from all forms of violence, psychological pressure, and degradation of dignity, both physical and psychological.
Torture, cruel, inhuman, or degrading treatment or punishment is prohibited under all circumstances, including in times of war, states of emergency, or threats to security.
No exceptional circumstances, including orders from public officials, security threats, counter-terrorism efforts, or states of emergency, may be used as justification for the application of torture or cruel treatment. Violations of this principle shall incur legal responsibility at both the national and international levels, including actions against state and non-state actors involved in torture.
Any detention, imprisonment, or other restriction of freedom must be carried out in conditions that exclude cruel or degrading treatment. Persons in custody have the right to humane treatment and respect for their dignity.
No one shall be subjected to enforced disappearance. States are obligated to take effective measures to prevent such crimes and to ensure the investigation of all cases of enforced disappearances.
No one shall be subjected to medical, biological, or scientific experiments without their voluntary, informed, and freely given consent. Forced experimentation is prohibited.
States are required to conduct prompt, thorough, and impartial investigations into all cases of alleged torture, cruel treatment, and enforced disappearances. Perpetrators must be held accountable, regardless of their official position.
Victims of torture and cruel treatment have the right to fair compensation, including financial assistance, psychological support, and full rehabilitation.
States are required to cooperate with international human rights bodies and provide information about compliance with the prohibition of torture and inhuman treatment. Failure to cooperate may be considered a violation of international obligations.
States are required to conduct educational programs for law enforcement, military, and medical personnel to prevent torture and inhuman treatment, as well as to foster a culture of respect for human rights.

Article 15: Right to a Fair Trial and Legal Protection

Every person has the right to a fair, impartial, and public trial conducted by a competent and independent court, ensuring equality of the parties, access to justice, and protection from any unlawful interference.
Any person accused of a crime has the right to the presumption of innocence until proven guilty in accordance with the law. The prosecution is required to prove the guilt, and any doubts should be interpreted in favor of the accused. The standard of proof must adhere to international norms, and any evidence obtained illegally shall not be admissible.
Every person has the right to qualified legal assistance. If a person cannot afford a lawyer, the state must provide free legal aid to ensure justice and equality before the law.
During the judicial proceedings, equality of the parties shall be ensured. Each party has the right to present evidence, participate in the examination of witnesses, and use all lawful means to protect their interests.
Judicial proceedings should be open and accessible to the public, except when necessary for the protection of state secrets, public morals, the rights of minors, or other participants in the process, and in cases where the disclosure of information may threaten the safety of the parties.
No one shall be tried twice for the same offense (the principle of "ne bis in idem"). A new sentence may only be pronounced if new circumstances are discovered that significantly change the course of the case.
Every person has the right to appeal judgments and decisions of the courts to higher judicial authorities as prescribed by law. Appeal procedures must be available, fair, and ensure equality of the parties.
Judges are independent and subject only to the law. Any interference in the judiciary's activities, pressure on judges, or attempts to influence judicial decisions are unacceptable and shall be subject to criminal liability.
A person who does not speak the language of the proceedings has the right to free interpretation services at all stages of the trial, including preliminary hearings and appeals.
Every person has the right to approach international human rights bodies and courts if all effective legal remedies in the national jurisdiction have been exhausted.
Any evidence obtained in violation of human rights or through unlawful methods, including torture, threats, or coercion, shall be deemed inadmissible in court, in accordance with international justice standards.
Victims of crimes have the right to fair treatment, protection of their interests during the trial, access to information about the case, and the opportunity to participate in the process.

Article 16: Freedom of Thought, Conscience, Religion, and Worldview

Every person has the right to freedom of thought, including the right to form, express, and change their beliefs, as well as the freedom of conscience and worldview, without fear of persecution, pressure, or discrimination.
No one shall be compelled to act in a manner contrary to their conscience, worldview, or ethical beliefs. The right to freedom of conscience includes the right to refuse to perform duties that contradict personal moral principles, provided that the lawful interests of society and the rights of others are respected.
Every person has the right to profess any religion or worldview, or to profess none. This right includes the freedom to choose, change, and express their religious or philosophical beliefs either individually or collectively, publicly or privately.
Freedom of religion includes the right to perform religious rituals, holidays, worship services, religious education, establish religious organizations, and participate in them, provided that public order, the rights of others, and national standards are respected.
Member states recognize the equality of all religions and worldviews, without establishing a state religion. The state must maintain neutrality in matters of faith and belief, ensuring equality before the law for all religious and philosophical teachings.
No one shall be subjected to discrimination, persecution, or restriction of rights on the basis of their religious or non-religious beliefs. Incitement of hatred based on religious differences is prohibited, as well as using religion or worldview for purposes that threaten the rights of others.
No one shall be compelled to adopt a particular religion, participate in religious rituals, or renounce their beliefs. Coercion to change religious or philosophical beliefs is prohibited.
The Constitution protects the freedom of scientific inquiry, philosophical research, and critical thinking. No one shall be restricted in their right to develop and disseminate scientific knowledge, provided it does not threaten public safety, the rights of others, or violate legal norms.
States shall ensure the protection of the rights of religious and philosophical minorities, providing conditions for the preservation of their cultural and spiritual identity, and ensuring equality and protection of their rights.
Educational institutions shall ensure freedom of worldview views. Education should foster the development of critical thinking, respect for diversity, freedom of belief choice, and strengthen the principles of social justice.
Any restrictions on freedom of religion may only be established for the purpose of protecting public order, health, morals, or the basic rights and freedoms of others. Such restrictions must strictly comply with laws and international human rights standards and must be necessary and proportionate.

Article 17: Freedom of Speech, Information, and Expression

Every person has the right to freedom of expression, including the right to freely seek, receive, and disseminate information by any lawful means, without fear of persecution, retribution, or discrimination.
The media shall be free and independent from governmental or private pressures. Censorship, persecution of journalists, and restrictions on press freedom are categorically prohibited. States are obligated to protect the rights of journalists and ensure their safety, as well as create conditions for the independent operation of media outlets.
Every person has the right to access information held by public authorities and international organizations, except in cases where such access is contrary to the protection of state secrets, public safety, or the right to privacy of personal data.
Freedom of speech does not absolve individuals from responsibility for the dissemination of false, defamatory, hate-inciting, or violent speech. States are required to adopt laws to protect against abuses of freedom of speech, including false or harmful statements that undermine public order.
The promotion of war, violence, racial, religious, ethnic, or other forms of hate aimed at discrimination, incitement of hostility, or violence is prohibited and subject to legal responsibility.
Every person has the right to freedom of artistic, scientific, and cultural expression. Limitations may only be imposed in cases of threats to public order, morals, or the rights of others, in compliance with the principles of proportionality and necessity.
States are obligated to create conditions for the free exchange of ideas, opinions, and information, encouraging pluralism in the media and supporting the diversity of information sources, including independent and alternative media.
The use of media and technologies for mass manipulation of public consciousness, dissemination of disinformation, or undermining democratic institutions is prohibited and subject to legal consequences.
The right to freedom of speech and access to information extends to the digital space, including the internet and social media. Restrictions on access to digital platforms are only allowed in cases expressly provided by law, and for the purpose of protecting public interest, security, or the rights of others.
States are obligated to develop media literacy programs for all age groups, promoting critical information consumption and the conscious use of digital technologies, ensuring that citizens are able to adequately assess the sources and content of information.
Any restrictions on freedom of speech and information must be prescribed by law, necessary for a democratic society, and proportional to objectives such as protecting national security, public order, health, morals, and the rights of others.

Article 18: Right to Privacy and Protection of Personal Data

Every person has the right to respect for their private and family life, home, and correspondence, including the protection of personal and family secrets, as well as digital and biometric information. Interference with privacy may only occur on legal grounds.
Every person has the right to the protection of their personal data, including control over its collection, storage, processing, and dissemination, in compliance with the principles of confidentiality and cybersecurity. The processing of personal data may only take place with the consent of the data subject or based on legal grounds provided by law.
Public authorities, private companies, and other organizations are required to ensure the confidentiality of personal information and protect it from unauthorized access, leaks, alteration, or destruction.
No one may be subjected to unlawful surveillance, wiretapping of phone conversations, interception of electronic correspondence, or any other intrusion into their private life without a court decision or another legal basis.
The right to privacy extends to the digital realm, including the protection of personal data on the internet, social networks, and other electronic platforms. Every person has the right to protect their online communications from unauthorized access.
Every person has the right to access their personal data held by public authorities or private organizations, as well as the right to correct, update, or delete it if the data is no longer relevant or is used unlawfully.
States are required to adopt legislative and administrative measures to protect the right to privacy and personal data, including measures against cyber threats, data breaches, and unlawful monitoring. Additionally, states must create effective mechanisms for investigating violations and holding offenders accountable.
Restrictions on the right to privacy may only be established based on law and must be necessary and proportionate to achieve goals related to the protection of national security, public order, health, morals, or the rights of others.
The use of personal data for discrimination, manipulation, or limitation of rights and freedoms is prohibited and is subject to strict oversight by national and international authorities.
Every person has the right to request the deletion of their personal data from public sources if it is no longer relevant, infringes on the right to privacy, or was published unlawfully.
States are required to cooperate at the international level to ensure a high level of protection of personal data, exchange best practices, and harmonize standards for digital security, ensuring the protection of human rights on a global scale.

Article 19: Freedom of Movement and Choice of Residence

Every person has the right to move freely and choose their place of residence and abode within the territory of any state, provided that the rights and freedoms of others are respected.
Every person has the right to freely choose their place of residence both within their own country and abroad, subject to legal requirements regarding registration, security, and environmental protection.
Every person has the right to leave any country, including their own, and to return to their country without undue restrictions.
Restrictions on freedom of movement may only be imposed based on law and must be necessary and proportionate for the protection of national security, public order, public health, the rights and freedoms of others, and the protection of the environment.
Every person has the right to migrate to other countries in search of better living conditions, work, education, or asylum, subject to the immigration laws of the host countries and international human rights standards.
States must ensure the protection of the rights of internally displaced persons, including the right to safe return, integration into new communities, or relocation to other regions if necessary.
Every person who is subjected to persecution based on political, religious, ethnic, gender, cultural, or other grounds has the right to seek and enjoy asylum in other countries. States must respect international norms for the protection of refugees and asylum seekers.
No one shall be arbitrarily expelled or deported from their country. A decision to deport must be made based on law and be accompanied by the right to appeal to a court.
States must create conditions for the unhindered movement of persons with disabilities, including the development of accessible infrastructure and transport systems.
States must cooperate on matters related to the freedom of movement, the exchange of migration data, and the protection of the rights of migrants and refugees, while respecting the principles of humanity and the dignity of every individual.
Special attention must be given to the protection of the rights of individuals forced to leave their homes due to climate change, environmental disasters, and natural calamities. States must develop support and assistance mechanisms for such migrants.

Article 20: The Right to Citizenship and Its Alteration

Every person has the right to citizenship. No one shall be arbitrarily deprived of their citizenship or the right to change it.
States are obligated to take measures to prevent statelessness. Every person must have the right to legal affiliation with a state.
Every person has the right to freely choose or alter their citizenship, subject to the legal procedures provided by the laws of the relevant states.
The deprivation of citizenship may only occur in accordance with the law and for valid reasons, such as voluntary renunciation of citizenship or acquiring the citizenship of another state, as provided by law.
Individuals who have been unlawfully or unjustly deprived of their citizenship have the right to have it restored in an expedited manner.
Every child has the right to citizenship from birth. States must grant citizenship to children born on their territory if they would otherwise be stateless.
The procedure for acquiring citizenship through naturalization must be fair, transparent, and non-discriminatory, in line with the principles of respect for human rights and international standards.
States must respect the rights of individuals with dual or multiple citizenships and ensure their legal protection, except where it conflicts with international obligations.
Individuals residing outside their country have the right to protection and assistance from the diplomatic and consular institutions of their state.
States are obligated to protect the rights of stateless individuals, grant them legal status, and assist in their naturalization or legalization for integration into society.
The deprivation of citizenship cannot be used as a form of punishment for exercising fundamental rights and freedoms. Citizenship must not be used as a tool for discrimination or political pressure.
States must cooperate to prevent cases of statelessness, exchange information on the legal status of citizens, and ensure the protection of individuals at risk of losing their citizenship for political, ethnic, or other reasons.

Article 21: The Right to Participate in Governance and Political Life

Every person has the right to participate in the affairs of society and government, both directly and through freely elected representatives, in accordance with the principles of democracy and equality.
All citizens have equal access to public office and political positions, regardless of gender, race, ethnic origin, religion, social status, political views, or other characteristics.
The right to vote and be elected is granted to citizens who have reached the prescribed age, provided they have completed a basic course in civic education aimed at understanding the principles of democracy, governance, human rights, and ethical standards. This right includes the obligation to be aware of the social responsibility of one's choice.
Elections must be held regularly, based on universal and equal suffrage, with adherence to the principles of fairness, transparency, freedom, and competitiveness. Any form of falsification, coercion, or manipulation is prohibited.
Every person has the right to form political parties, movements, and organizations, as well as to participate in their activities. Restrictions may only apply in cases provided by law and aimed at protecting democratic order and the rights of others.
Every person has the right to freely express their political views, participate in public debates, and engage in political criticism without fear of persecution or discrimination.
No one may be restricted in their rights and freedoms based on political beliefs. Discrimination on political grounds is prohibited and subject to legal accountability.
States are obligated to take measures to ensure equal participation of women and men in political life, including representation in governing bodies and decision-making.
Youth have the right to participate in political life, including the opportunity to be elected to public and state offices in accordance with established age requirements and upon completion of civic education.
Public authorities must act transparently and be accountable to society. Public oversight of government activities is an integral part of democratic governance.
States must cooperate internationally to strengthen democratic institutions, exchange best practices, and promote free and fair elections worldwide.
To obtain the right to vote, citizens must undergo a civic education program that includes the basics of ethics, law, governance, and social development. This ensures that voters make informed decisions that contribute to the well-being of society.

Article 22: The Right to Peaceful Assembly and Association

Every person has the right to freedom of peaceful assembly, including rallies, demonstrations, pickets, and other forms of collective expression of opinion. This right may be exercised both individually and as part of groups, organizations, or movements.
Every person has the right to freely form and join public associations, trade unions, political parties, and other organizations to protect their interests, promote ideas, and participate in public and political life.
Restrictions on the holding of peaceful assemblies and the formation of associations are permissible only on the basis of law and in cases where it is necessary to protect national security, public order, health, morality, or the rights and freedoms of others.
Public authorities are obligated to protect participants in peaceful assemblies from violence, persecution, and unlawful actions by third parties. The use of force against peaceful demonstrations is permissible only in cases of extreme necessity and must be proportionate.
No one may be subjected to prosecution, repression, or discrimination for participating in peaceful assemblies or membership in lawful associations.
The rules and procedures concerning the organization and conduct of assemblies must be clearly defined in legislation, simple, and accessible for understanding by all citizens.
States must ensure special conditions for the exercise of the right to peaceful assembly and association for vulnerable groups, including people with disabilities, national minorities, and other social categories.
The right to form and participate in international associations and movements is recognized and protected. States may not unjustifiably restrict their citizens' participation in international organizations, provided their activities do not violate international law.
Any unlawful interference with the right to peaceful assembly and freedom of association shall be thoroughly investigated, and those responsible shall be held accountable in accordance with international human rights standards.
The freedom of association extends to the digital space. States must ensure the freedom to create online communities and hold virtual assemblies, provided that ethical norms and public safety standards are observed.

Article 23: The Right to Work and Fair Working Conditions

Every person has the right to work in conditions of freedom, fairness, and dignity. This right includes the freedom to choose a profession, fair working conditions, and protection against unemployment.
Every person has the right to safe, hygienic, and decent working conditions, including protection from exploitation, discrimination, and forced labor.
Men and women performing the same work have the right to equal pay for equal work, without any discrimination based on gender, age, race, religion, or other factors.
Every worker has the right to a fair and adequate wage that ensures a decent standard of living for them and their family, including the ability to meet basic needs.
Forced or compulsory labor is prohibited in all forms, except in cases provided for by international law (such as military service or the fulfillment of community duties).
Every person has the right to reasonable limitations on working hours, regular paid vacations, and rest days. States must ensure compliance with working hour regulations and the right to rest.
Every worker has the right to form and join trade unions to protect their rights and interests. Trade unions have the right to engage in collective bargaining and participate in decision-making processes concerning working conditions.
Workers have the right to participate in collective bargaining with employers and, if necessary, to engage in peaceful strikes to defend their economic and social interests.
Special attention shall be given to the protection of workers from vulnerable groups, including women, youth, persons with disabilities, and migrants, to prevent discrimination and exploitation.
States are obligated to take measures to ensure full and productive employment, the creation of new jobs, the development of vocational training, and support for small and medium-sized businesses.
Every worker has the right to social protection, including insurance against accidents, illness, disability, maternity, old age, and unemployment.
States must ensure the protection of labor rights in the context of the digital economy, including the rights of freelancers, remote workers, and platform-based workers.
States shall comply with international labor standards, cooperate with international organizations, and implement best practices to ensure decent working conditions worldwide.

Article 24: The Right to Social Protection and Welfare

Every person has the right to social protection, including access to essential social services and support systems necessary to ensure a decent standard of living.
States are obligated to create and maintain effective social protection systems covering all citizens, including the unemployed, elderly, persons with disabilities, children, migrants, and other vulnerable groups.
Every person has the right to a guaranteed basic income sufficient to meet basic needs such as food, clothing, housing, healthcare, and education.
All citizens have the right to social insurance, including insurance for illness, disability, accidents, old age, maternity, and unemployment.
States are required to provide special support measures for families, including child allowances, access to quality education and healthcare, and protection of children's rights.
States are obligated to take measures to prevent and reduce poverty, ensure social justice, and provide equal access to resources and opportunities for all members of society.
Every person has the right to access quality social services, including emergency assistance, crisis support, and social integration programs.
States are required to ensure a decent standard of living for elderly persons, including pensions, healthcare, care services, and active aging programs.
Social policies must be inclusive and consider the needs of various population groups, promoting equality and social integration.
States are responsible for the development and implementation of effective social protection programs, including monitoring systems and assessing their effectiveness.
States are obligated to cooperate internationally to exchange experiences and best practices in the field of social protection and welfare, as well as to assist countries with limited resources.
States must adopt special measures to ensure social protection during economic, environmental, and humanitarian crises, providing prompt and effective assistance to those affected.

Article 25: The Right to Health and Medical Care

Every person has an inalienable right to the highest attainable standard of physical and mental health. This right includes access to quality medical services and conditions conducive to a healthy lifestyle.
Every person has the right to timely, accessible, and quality medical care without discrimination on any grounds, including social status, economic condition, gender, age, race, ethnic origin, or place of residence.
States are obligated to provide a basic package of medical services, including preventive, curative, palliative, and rehabilitative care, as well as mental health services.
States must take measures for disease prevention, including vaccination programs, promoting healthy lifestyles, monitoring public health, and combating epidemics.
Every person has the right to access safe, effective, and affordable medications. States must regulate the pharmaceutical market and prevent speculation on medical products.
In emergency situations, including natural disasters, epidemics, and humanitarian crises, states must ensure emergency access to medical care for all those in need.
Every person has the right to access information and services regarding reproductive health, including family planning, maternal health, and safe childbirth conditions.
States must pay special attention to protecting the health of vulnerable groups, including children, the elderly, persons with disabilities, migrants, and refugees.
Every person has the right to complete information about their health and to informed consent before undergoing any medical intervention.
No one may be subjected to compulsory medical treatment or medical experiments without their free and informed consent, except in cases provided by law and in the interest of public health.
States are obligated to establish standards for the quality and safety of medical care, as well as to monitor the activities of medical institutions and professionals.
States must develop a mental health system, including prevention, treatment, and rehabilitation of mental disorders, as well as combatting the stigma surrounding mental illnesses.
States must cooperate internationally to exchange experiences, scientific knowledge, and technologies in the field of healthcare, as well as to respond collectively to global health threats.
States must take into account the impact of the environment on public health and take measures to reduce environmental risks, including controlling air, water, and soil pollution.

Article 26: The Right to Education and Cultural Development

Every person has an inalienable right to education, which should be accessible, free at the primary and basic levels, and aimed at the comprehensive development of the individual, respect for human rights, and fundamental freedoms.
Education must be accessible to all, without discrimination on the grounds of gender, race, language, religion, social or economic status, disability, or any other factor, ensuring equal opportunities for everyone.
States are obligated to ensure high-quality education, develop critical thinking, creative abilities, scientific knowledge, and ethical values in accordance with the principles of Neohumanism.
Every person has the right to freely choose an educational institution and form of education, including alternative and informal educational programs that suit their needs and interests.
Primary education should be compulsory and free for all. States must ensure its availability, especially for vulnerable groups, and create conditions for equal access to all educational resources.
Secondary education must be publicly available and free within the limits set by law. Higher education should be accessible based on the merits and abilities of the students, providing access to quality education for all social strata.
Every person has the right to continuous education and vocational training throughout life, to develop their skills and adapt to changes in society and the labor market.
States are obligated to provide inclusive education that takes into account the needs of people with disabilities, ethnic minorities, and other vulnerable groups.
Every person has the right to participate in the cultural life of society, access achievements in science, art, and literature, as well as to contribute to the preservation and development of humanity's cultural heritage.
States are obligated to protect and support cultural heritage, including historical monuments, traditions, languages, and customs, and ensure their transmission to future generations, promoting the development of cultural identity.
Every person has the right to freedom of artistic, scientific, and literary creativity, without censorship, except when necessary for public safety, the protection of morals, or the rights of others, and in strict accordance with the law.
States are obligated to foster intercultural dialogue, mutual respect, and cooperation among peoples and cultures, strengthening solidarity, tolerance, and diversity.
Educational programs should place particular emphasis on human rights, sustainable development, ecology, and global responsibility, forming conscious citizens capable of acting in the interests of all humanity.
States are obligated to cooperate internationally in the fields of education and culture, facilitating the exchange of knowledge, scientific research, and cultural experiences, to strengthen mutual understanding, solidarity, and peace among nations.

Article 27: The Right to Adequate Housing and Living Conditions

Every person has the right to decent, safe, affordable, and habitable housing, providing protection from adverse climatic conditions, health threats, and ensuring social security.
States are obligated to develop and implement policies aimed at ensuring the availability of housing for all, especially for socially vulnerable groups, including low-income families, the elderly, people with disabilities, and the homeless.
No one may be arbitrarily deprived of their housing. Eviction is only permitted on lawful grounds, with the provision of alternative housing and under conditions that guarantee the respect of human dignity.
States must establish and maintain standards for the quality and safety of housing, ensuring access to clean drinking water, sanitation, electricity, and heating systems.
The right to decent housing includes access to infrastructure such as roads, public transportation, schools, medical facilities, and other social services necessary for the proper functioning of society.
States are obligated to protect the rights of tenants, including protection from unfair rental conditions, unauthorized evictions, and excessive rent rates.
States must encourage sustainable housing construction that meets ecological standards, principles of energy efficiency, and the use of renewable resources.
In cases of natural disasters, armed conflicts, or other emergencies, states must provide emergency housing for those affected.
Every person has the right to participate in decisions related to the management of housing, particularly in apartment buildings and residential communities, to ensure equality and fairness in the distribution of resources.
States must cooperate at the international level to exchange experiences and best practices in ensuring the right to adequate housing and improving living conditions worldwide.
Minimum standards for living space per person are established to ensure comfortable living conditions and to meet hygiene requirements.
States must ensure the accessibility of housing and infrastructure for people with disabilities, including architectural and technological solutions that provide equal opportunities for all.
The right to own, use, and dispose of housing is affirmed, subject to the public interest, the rights of others, and compliance with environmental and social standards.
Fair rules for the real estate market are established to prevent speculation, artificial price inflation, and concentration of property in the hands of a few, ensuring equality and accessibility of housing for all.
Government and municipal social housing programs are established to ensure housing availability for low-income groups and those in socially vulnerable positions.
States must develop and implement programs for the reconstruction and modernization of outdated housing stock to improve quality of life, enhance energy efficiency, and comply with environmental standards.
The introduction of ecological standards in housing construction, including the use of environmentally friendly materials and energy-saving technologies, must become mandatory.
Protection of the rights of citizens who own or rent housing abroad is ensured in accordance with international legal norms, guaranteeing equality of rights for all.
Effective mechanisms are created to protect housing rights, including the ability to file complaints and appeals to the court, as well as the creation of independent bodies to oversee the enforcement of housing rights.
Educational programs are promoted to raise citizens' awareness of their rights and responsibilities related to housing and to encourage responsible ownership and use of residential property, including adherence to safety and sustainability standards.

Article 28: The Right to Food Security and Access to Clean Water

Every person has the inalienable right to an adequate amount of nutritious, safe, and culturally acceptable food, necessary for maintaining health and ensuring full physical and mental development.
States are obligated to ensure the availability of food for all, especially for socially vulnerable groups, including children, the elderly, people with disabilities, and individuals in emergency situations, considering nutritional needs and cultural preferences.
States must regulate the standards of food quality and safety, preventing contamination, falsification, and other health threats to the population, ensuring high standards of food security.
To ensure the right to food, states must develop sustainable agricultural practices, support local producers and agricultural cooperatives, and promote food independence and resilience to external crises.
States are obligated to develop and implement programs to prevent and eliminate hunger, malnutrition, and nutrient deficiencies among the population, with special attention to ensuring food security during crises.
Every person has the right to access clean, safe, and affordable drinking water to meet basic needs, including drinking, food preparation, personal hygiene, and sanitation.
States must ensure the rational and sustainable management of water resources, prevent pollution and depletion of freshwater sources, and take measures to protect ecosystems and maintain water resources.
Special attention must be given to ensuring access to food and water for individuals in humanitarian crises, armed conflicts, natural disasters, and environmental catastrophes, as well as for vulnerable groups such as refugees and internally displaced persons.
States must promote educational programs aimed at raising awareness about healthy eating, rational use of natural resources, water hygiene, and sustainable consumption.
States are obligated to cooperate at the international level to combat global hunger, exchange best practices in food security and sustainable water management, with the goal of ensuring the availability of food and water for all people on the planet.

Article 29: The Right to Clothing and Decent Living Conditions

Every person has the right to access sufficient, quality, and climate-appropriate clothing that provides protection and is suitable for their living conditions.
States are obligated to take measures to ensure the availability of clothing for all population groups, especially for vulnerable groups, including children, the elderly, people with disabilities, and other socially vulnerable groups.
States should encourage ethical clothing production, respect for labor rights in the textile and garment industry, and the sustainable use of resources, including those necessary for the production of clothing.
Every person has the right to decent living conditions, including access to essential services such as clean water, sanitation, energy supply, heating, and personal hygiene products, which contribute to maintaining health and well-being.
States are obligated to establish minimum hygiene standards to ensure the health and safety of the population, particularly in densely populated urban areas, temporary housing, and social welfare institutions.
In times of crisis, natural disasters, armed conflicts, or other emergencies, states are obligated to provide access to clothing and necessary household resources for the affected, including temporary housing, sanitary conditions, and basic essentials.
States should encourage the use of eco-friendly materials, the recycling and reuse of clothing and household items to minimize environmental impact and promote sustainable consumption.
Every person has the right to equal access to resources necessary for decent living conditions, without discrimination based on any grounds, including social status, gender, age, disability, ethnic origin, or any other factors.
States are obligated to ensure access to information on sustainable consumption practices, hygiene, and the rational use of resources to raise public awareness of the most effective and environmentally safe ways to improve quality of life.
States are obligated to cooperate at the international level to exchange best practices in ensuring the right to clothing and decent living conditions, and provide assistance to countries and regions facing resource shortages, ensuring the availability of essential services and goods for all.

Article 30: The Right to a Safe and Sustainable Environment

Every person has the right to live in a clean, healthy, and sustainable environment that promotes well-being and health, ensuring access to clean water, air, and non-toxic food.
States are obligated to develop and implement policies aimed at preventing pollution of air, water, and soil, reducing greenhouse gas emissions, and protecting natural resources. Special attention is given to the preservation of forests, water resources, biodiversity, and the protection of ecosystems.
State policies must aim at achieving sustainable development, including the rational use of natural resources, transitioning to renewable energy sources, minimizing waste, and reducing environmental impact to preserve the planet for future generations.
Every person has the right to timely and full access to information regarding the state of the environment, environmental risks, pollution, and measures taken to mitigate them. States are obligated to ensure transparency of environmental information and provide data on the impact on nature.
Citizens have the right to participate in decision-making on environmental issues, including through public hearings, consultations, and initiatives related to environmental protection. States must ensure the accessibility of mechanisms for participation in environmental processes.
In the event of natural and man-made disasters, states are obligated to respond promptly, ensure the protection of public health, access to clean water, food, and medical assistance, as well as carry out recovery efforts aimed at restoring natural ecosystems.
Every person has the right to access judicial and administrative mechanisms to protect their environmental rights, including the possibility of appealing to international bodies when effective national environmental protection mechanisms are unavailable.
States are obligated to implement environmental education programs at all levels, raising public awareness about the importance of nature conservation, sustainable consumption, combating climate change, and maintaining ecological balance.
States must cooperate at the international level to address global environmental issues, exchange environmental technologies, coordinate measures to combat climate change, protect transboundary ecosystems, and promote sustainable development.
Individuals, organizations, and states that cause harm to the environment bear full legal responsibility, including the obligation to compensate for the damage caused, restore ecosystems to their original state, and take measures to prevent further harm.

Article 31: The Right to Sanitary Conditions and Hygiene

Every person has the right to access safe, hygienic, and sanitary conditions, including clean toilets, personal hygiene products, and sanitary infrastructure, which are necessary for the preservation of health and well-being.
States are obligated to ensure proper sanitary conditions in residential, public, and workspaces, promoting disease prevention and safeguarding public health. These conditions must meet international standards and national sanitary regulations.
Every person has the right to access quality sanitary infrastructure, including water supply systems, sewage systems, and waste disposal, which are essential for maintaining high levels of hygiene and health.
States are obligated to promote the dissemination of knowledge about hygienic practices and disease prevention measures at all levels of education, starting from preschool education and continuing through vocational training.
Special attention must be given to ensuring sanitary conditions for vulnerable groups, including children, elderly people, individuals with disabilities, migrants, refugees, and people living in extreme poverty.
In emergency situations, including natural disasters, epidemics, and humanitarian crises, states are obligated to ensure emergency access to sanitary resources and hygiene products, including mobile sanitation facilities and preventive measures.
States are obligated to regularly monitor sanitary conditions and take measures to prevent sanitary crises, including preventive campaigns and prompt responses to threats.
Sanitary measures must consider ecological aspects, including rational and sustainable waste management, reducing pollution, and preserving water resources, to minimize environmental impact.
States must adhere to international standards in sanitation and hygiene, cooperating to exchange best practices, technologies, and ensuring unified approaches to solving sanitation problems on a global scale.
Individuals and organizations that violate sanitary regulations, creating a threat to public health, shall be held accountable in accordance with national and international laws, including obligations to restore and compensate for damages.




Article 32: The Right to Energy and Utility Resources

Every person has the right to access clean, sustainable, and affordable energy sources necessary to meet basic needs and ensure a decent standard of living, including heating, electricity, and access to renewable energy sources.
Energy resources, especially renewable energy sources, should be under public control or managed by cooperatives to prevent monopolization and uneven distribution that contribute to social inequality.
States are obligated to ensure fair and equal access to energy resources for all, especially for vulnerable and low-income groups, including retirees, people with disabilities, and low-income families.
The development of energy infrastructure should be based on principles of environmental sustainability, minimizing environmental impact, complying with energy efficiency standards, and transitioning to renewable energy sources that contribute to the long-term sustainability of the planet.
Every person has the right to access quality utility services, including water supply, sanitation, heating, and electricity, which must be provided on fair and affordable terms, with due consideration for social and environmental responsibility.
States are obligated to develop and promote energy efficiency policies, including programs for reducing energy consumption, stimulating the use of eco-friendly technologies, and supporting green initiatives and startups in the field of sustainable technologies.
States and international organizations should invest in the research and development of new energy technologies that promote sustainable development, improve quality of life, and protect the environment.
To ensure global energy security, international cooperation is required, including the exchange of knowledge, technologies, and joint programs for the development of sustainable energy systems, as well as the harmonization of standards to ensure equal conditions at the international level.
Individuals, organizations, and states that violate the principles of sustainability, social justice, and environmental responsibility in the energy sector shall be held legally accountable for the environmental and social harm caused, including obligations to restore the damage and compensate the affected parties.
Consumers' rights to fair tariffs, quality services, and protection against abuse in the energy and utility sectors must be guaranteed by law, and mechanisms for their protection and dispute resolution must be provided.

Article 33: The Right to Sustainable Development, Environmental Justice, and Rational Use of Natural Resources

Every person has the inalienable right to sustainable development, which includes equal access to natural resources, environmentally safe technologies, and living conditions that foster harmonious coexistence with the natural environment.
Environmental justice is based on equality of access to clean air, water, fertile soils, and other vital resources, regardless of social, economic, or geographical status. No one can be deprived of the right to a healthy environment.
Strategically important resources, including water, forests, mineral wealth, and other natural resources, must be under public ownership or controlled by democratically elected bodies to prevent their exploitation for the benefit of narrow groups.
States are obligated to ensure equal and fair access to natural resources. Local communities have the right to participate in the management and decision-making regarding the use of resources in their territories.
The use of natural resources must be based on principles of rationality and sustainability, considering their renewability and the necessity of preserving them for future generations. Economic activities must minimize environmental impact.
Sustainable development is impossible without the preservation of biodiversity and protection of ecosystems. States are obligated to take measures to protect endangered species, maintain ecological balance, and restore damaged ecosystems.
All projects related to the use of natural resources must meet strict environmental standards. States are obligated to stimulate the development and implementation of environmentally friendly technologies and innovations that promote sustainable development.
States are obligated to develop and implement policies for the rational use of natural resources, preventing their depletion, environmental degradation, and loss of biodiversity. Special attention is given to renewable resources and environmentally sustainable technologies.
Individuals, organizations, and states that harm the environment are obligated to compensate for the damage caused and take measures to restore ecosystems. Priority is given to restoring natural processes and biodiversity.
States are obligated to cooperate internationally to address global environmental issues, exchange advanced technologies, and jointly protect transboundary natural resources.
States are obligated to promote environmental education, raise public awareness about the importance of sustainable development, and foster environmentally responsible behavior.
In cases of scientific uncertainty regarding potential harm to the environment or human health, precautionary measures must be taken to prevent possible damage.
Individuals and organizations that harm the environment bear legal and material responsibility for the damage caused, including obligations to restore damaged ecosystems and compensate those affected.
Every person has the right to participate in decisions regarding environmental protection issues, including access to environmental information, participation in public consultations, and the right to judicial protection of environmental rights.

Article 34. Economic Democracy and Fair Distribution of Resources

Economic democracy is based on the principles of social justice and aims to ensure equal opportunities for all individuals in accessing economic resources, means of production, and goods. Its main goal is to eliminate economic inequality and create conditions for the comprehensive development of each individual and society as a whole.
Strategically important sectors of the economy, natural resources, and infrastructure must be in public or cooperative ownership. This is necessary to prevent monopolization and ensure fair distribution of income and resources. The management of these resources is carried out by decentralized structures that reflect the interests of local communities.
All forms of economic exploitation, including unfair trade, monopolistic practices, and corruption schemes, are prohibited. States are obliged to take measures to prevent such practices and ensure fair competition.
States are obliged to promote the development of cooperative enterprises and community initiatives that contribute to economic independence, social responsibility, and sustainable development of local communities.
Every individual has the right to access land, water, energy resources, and other basic means of production. States must create conditions for the fair distribution of these resources, preventing their concentration in the hands of a few.
States are obliged to develop and implement strategies for sustainable economic development aimed at reducing inequality, creating jobs, and ensuring economic stability. Economic plans should be based on the principles of fairness, sustainability, and social responsibility.
Economic activity should be carried out with due regard for social responsibility, respect for workers' rights, environmental protection, and adherence to ethical standards. States are obliged to establish standards for social responsibility and monitor their enforcement.
Every individual has the right to participate in the economic life of society, access economic opportunities, and receive fair compensation for their work. States must ensure equal conditions for all, regardless of social, economic, or cultural background.
States are obliged to cooperate in creating a fair global economic system based on the principles of solidarity, equality, and respect for the economic rights of all peoples. International agreements should promote sustainable development and reduce global economic inequality.
Individuals and organizations that violate economic rights bear legal and financial responsibility. States are obliged to provide effective mechanisms for protecting economic rights and ensuring fair distribution of resources.

Article 35. Right to Participate in Cooperatives and Community Initiatives

The right to participate in cooperatives and community initiatives is an integral part of economic democracy. Cooperative and community-based governance models promote fair resource distribution, economic self-sufficiency, and sustainable development of local communities.
Every individual has the right to establish and participate in cooperatives, regardless of social, economic, or cultural status. This right includes involvement in decision-making, resource management, and profit distribution.
States must support the development of various forms of cooperatives, including production, consumer, agricultural, housing, educational, and financial cooperatives. Cooperatives can operate at local, national, and international levels.
States must create favorable conditions for the development of cooperative structures by providing tax incentives, access to financing, educational programs, and technical assistance. Cooperative ownership must be legally recognized and protected.
Cooperatives must be managed on the principles of democracy and equality. Every member has the right to vote, regardless of the size of their contribution or share in the capital.
Cooperatives must operate based on the principles of sustainable development, taking into account ecological factors and promoting social responsibility. Cooperative members must adhere to ethical standards and principles of fair trade.
States must provide legal protection for cooperative members, including protection against unfair practices, discrimination, and economic exploitation. Members have the right to fair profit distribution and participation in decision-making processes.
Every individual has the right to participate in community initiatives aimed at local development, environmental protection, cultural enrichment, and social support. States must support such initiatives and ensure conditions for their long-term sustainability.
States must promote the development of international cooperative networks, facilitating knowledge exchange and best practices. International cooperative cooperation contributes to global economic justice and strengthens solidarity among nations.
Individuals and organizations that violate the rights to participate in cooperatives and community initiatives shall bear legal responsibility. States must provide effective mechanisms for the protection of these rights and support affected parties.

Article 36. Recognition of Nature as a Subject of Rights

Nature is recognized as a subject of rights, possessing inherent dignity and value, independent of its utility to humans. This article is based on the principles of Neohumanism, affirming the interconnectedness of all forms of life and the human responsibility for environmental preservation.
All elements of nature, including ecosystems, species of flora and fauna, water, and air resources, have an inalienable right to exist, develop, and restore their natural cycles and processes.
Ecosystems have the right to maintain their integrity, biodiversity, and resilience. Any activity that violates these rights is subject to restrictions or prohibition.
States are obliged to develop and implement legal mechanisms to protect the rights of nature, including the creation of environmental justice institutions authorized to defend the interests of nature in court.
Every individual and community has the right to participate in the protection of nature's rights, including the right to approach the court on behalf of nature to prevent environmental harm or restore damaged ecosystems.
In the absence of full scientific certainty regarding potential harm to the environment, precautionary measures must be taken to prevent damage.
States and private entities that cause harm to nature are obligated to restore ecosystems and compensate for environmental damage. Priority should be given to restoring the natural state of the environment.
States must promote the development of environmental education, fostering a culture of respect for nature and awareness of its rights.
States must collaborate at the international level to protect the rights of nature, safeguard global ecosystems, and address transboundary environmental threats.
Individuals, organizations, and states that violate the rights of nature bear legal and financial responsibility, including obligations to prevent, eliminate, and compensate for environmental damage.

Article 37. Rights of Animals and Protection of Biodiversity

All living beings possess inherent value and the right to respect, irrespective of their utility to humans. The rights of animals and the protection of biodiversity are based on the principles of Neohumanism, which recognize the interdependence of all forms of life on the planet.
Animals have the right to live free from cruelty, exploitation, and unnecessary suffering. This right includes protection from violence, abuse, trafficking, and use for purposes that harm their physical or mental well-being.
States are obligated to take measures to preserve and restore natural ecosystems that serve as habitats for wildlife and plants. The destruction of habitats, pollution, and unsustainable use of natural resources must be strictly regulated.
The use of animals in agriculture, science, education, and the entertainment industry must be strictly regulated to ensure compliance with ethical standards and minimize suffering. Preference should be given to alternative methods that do not involve the exploitation of animals.
States must develop and implement biodiversity protection programs, including measures for the protection of rare and endangered species, the prevention of poaching, and illegal wildlife trade.
Every person has the right to participate in initiatives aimed at protecting animals and preserving biodiversity, including participation in environmental movements, human rights organizations, and public actions.
For effective protection of animals and biodiversity, international cooperation, exchange of best practices, and coordination of efforts within global environmental agreements are necessary.
Individuals and organizations involved in the abuse of animals, the destruction of biodiversity, or illegal wildlife trade shall bear legal and financial responsibility. States are required to provide effective monitoring and enforcement mechanisms for such violations.
States must promote environmental education, fostering respect for animals and awareness of the importance of biodiversity for a sustainable future of the planet.

Article 38. The Right of Peoples to Self-Determination and Cultural Autonomy

The right of peoples to self-determination is a fundamental right recognized by the international community. It includes the freedom of peoples to determine their political status, economic development, social, and cultural policies without external interference.
Every people has the right to freely choose their form of governance, including the possibility of establishing an independent state, autonomy, or other political organization within existing states, provided that the principles of peaceful coexistence are respected.
Peoples and ethnic groups have the right to preserve, develop, and pass on their cultural identity, language, traditions, and customs. States are obligated to respect and protect cultural diversity, preventing discrimination based on ethnicity and culture.
The right to self-determination includes economic independence, allowing peoples to manage their resources and determine economic policies that align with their needs and interests.
Peoples have the right to participate in governance at all levels, including representation in governmental bodies and international organizations. This right guarantees participation in decision-making processes that affect their interests.
Indigenous peoples have special rights to protect their land, natural resources, traditional way of life, and cultural practices. States must recognize and protect these rights, including the right to consultation and participation in decisions affecting their territories.
All disputes related to the implementation of the right to self-determination must be resolved exclusively by peaceful means, based on dialogue, negotiations, and international law.
States are obligated to cooperate in promoting the implementation of the right to self-determination, respecting the sovereignty and territorial integrity of other states, and fostering the development of cultural diversity.
Any form of discrimination based on cultural, linguistic, ethnic, religious, or traditional grounds is categorically prohibited. States and international organizations are required to take measures to prevent cultural discrimination and oppression.
States and individuals who violate the right of peoples to self-determination shall bear legal responsibility in accordance with international law. International institutions must ensure the protection of these rights and support affected communities.

Article 39. Rights of Indigenous Peoples and Ethnic Minorities

Indigenous peoples and ethnic minorities possess the inalienable right to self-determination, the preservation of their cultural identity, traditions, languages, and way of life. These rights form the foundation for recognizing the value of cultural diversity and the necessity of development, in accordance with the principles of Neohumanism.
Every individual and group has the right to independently determine their ethnic, cultural, linguistic, and religious affiliation without external pressure or interference.
States are obligated to recognize and respect the cultural heritage of indigenous peoples and ethnic minorities, ensuring conditions for the preservation of their traditions, customs, languages, and arts.
Indigenous peoples have the right to own, use, and manage traditional lands, territories, and natural resources. States are obligated to guarantee the protection of these rights, preventing illegal land appropriation.
Indigenous peoples and ethnic minorities have the right to participate in decision-making processes concerning their rights, resources, and way of life, including the right to consultation and obtaining free, prior, and informed consent.
States are obligated to ensure access to education in the native languages of indigenous peoples and ethnic minorities, and to promote the development of educational programs that consider their history, traditions, and cultural characteristics.
Indigenous peoples and ethnic minorities have the right to equal access to economic opportunities, social services, healthcare, and decent working conditions, while taking into account their cultural characteristics.
Any form of discrimination, violence, forced assimilation, or violation of the rights of indigenous peoples and ethnic minorities is strictly prohibited. States are obligated to take measures to prevent such violations and ensure justice.
States are obligated to cooperate at the international level to protect the rights of indigenous peoples and ethnic minorities, exchange best practices, and implement international standards in this field.
Individuals, organizations, and states that violate the rights of indigenous peoples and ethnic minorities shall bear legal responsibility. States are obligated to provide effective mechanisms for the protection of these rights and the restoration of justice for affected communities.

Article 40. The Right to Preserve and Develop Cultural Heritage

Cultural heritage is an integral part of human civilization and encompasses a collection of material and immaterial values, including traditions, customs, languages, arts, architecture, and spiritual practices. This heritage contributes to the formation of national identities and strengthens global solidarity.
Every people and individual has the right to preserve, develop, and pass on their cultural identity, including traditions, customs, language, arts, and forms of spiritual expression.
States are obligated to take measures to protect and safeguard cultural heritage, including historical monuments, archaeological sites, artworks, traditional crafts, and oral traditions. This includes preventing illegal trade in cultural property and the destruction of culturally significant objects.
Every person has the right to access cultural goods, museums, libraries, archives, and other institutions that promote cultural development and education.
States are obligated to foster the development of cultural diversity by supporting initiatives aimed at preserving and revitalizing endangered languages, traditions, and art forms.
To strengthen mutual understanding and tolerance, states should promote intercultural dialogue, the exchange of cultural values, and collaborative projects aimed at developing cultural connections between peoples.
In the digital age, states are obligated to use modern technologies to preserve and disseminate cultural values, ensuring their accessibility for future generations.
States are obligated to integrate cultural heritage issues into educational programs, instilling in young people respect for cultural values and an understanding of their importance for the sustainable development of society.
States are obligated to cooperate at the international level to protect cultural heritage, exchange experiences, and implement joint projects aimed at preserving and developing the cultural heritage of humanity.
Individuals, organizations, and states involved in the destruction or illegal trade of cultural property shall bear legal responsibility. States are obligated to provide mechanisms for investigating and punishing such crimes, as well as for the restoration of lost heritage.

Article 42. The Right to Spiritual Development and Inner Freedom

Every person has the inalienable right to spiritual development and inner freedom, including the freedom of worldview, philosophical beliefs, and self-realization. This right is based on the principles of Neohumanism, which recognize the importance of spiritual and moral growth for the harmonious development of individuals and society.
Every person has the right to freedom of thought, conscience, and beliefs without fear of persecution or discrimination. This includes the freedom to form, express, and change oneâ€™s views, as well as the right to refuse actions that contradict personal moral principles.
States are obligated to promote the development of spiritual values, such as compassion, honesty, justice, and respect for the dignity of every person. Educational programs must include elements of ethical education and the formation of moral responsibility.
Every person has the right to the freedom of scientific inquiry, philosophical research, and critical thinking. Limitations may only be imposed to protect public safety, health, morality, or the rights of others.
States are obligated to create conditions for comprehensive personal growth, including access to education, culture, spiritual practices, and opportunities for self-realization.
No one may be forced to accept a particular religion, philosophy, or worldview, nor be compelled to participate in religious or spiritual practices against their will. Coercion to change oneâ€™s beliefs is prohibited.
States are obligated to respect and protect cultural and spiritual diversity, providing equal rights for all forms of spiritual expression, as long as they do not violate the rights of others.
Every person has the right to access spiritual and philosophical resources, including literature, educational programs, media, and cultural events that promote the development of inner peace and self-awareness.
States are obligated to cooperate at the international level to promote spiritual values, exchange cultural and philosophical experiences, and strengthen intercultural dialogue.
Individuals and organizations that violate the right to spiritual freedom shall bear legal responsibility. States are obligated to provide effective mechanisms for protecting this right and supporting those who have been harmed.



Article 43. Protection of Moral and Ethical Values in Society

Moral and ethical values in society are the foundation for building a just, harmonious, and sustainable world. These values include honesty, justice, compassion, respect for human dignity, and responsibility toward society and nature.
No ideology, religion, political theory, or cultural practice can be placed above universal moral principles. All laws and governmental decisions must be based on the highest moral standards, ensuring equality and justice for all.
States are obligated to develop educational programs aimed at fostering moral and ethical qualities in citizens, such as honesty, tolerance, responsibility, and respect for human rights. Ethical education should be integrated at all levels of education.
Respect for moral and ethical values implies the recognition and protection of cultural and spiritual diversity. States are obligated to create conditions for dialogue and mutual understanding between different cultures and traditions.
States are obligated to take measures to prevent the spread of ideologies, practices, and actions that undermine the moral foundations of society, including the promotion of violence, hatred, and discrimination.
All public institutions, including government bodies, educational establishments, and media, must be guided by ethical principles in their activities. This includes transparency, accountability, and respect for human rights.
States are obligated to cooperate at the international level to promote universal moral values, exchange best practices in ethical education, and strengthen moral standards globally.
Individuals, organizations, and states that violate moral and ethical norms shall bear legal and social responsibility. States are obligated to provide effective mechanisms for protecting moral values and supporting those harmed by moral violations.
Every person has the right to moral freedom of choice, provided that their actions do not infringe on the rights and freedoms of others. States are obligated to protect this right, creating conditions for the free expression of moral beliefs.
Civil society plays an important role in protecting and promoting moral and ethical values. States are obligated to support initiatives aimed at strengthening moral culture and ethical awareness in society.

Article 44. Protection of Moral and Ethical Values in Society

Moral and ethical values in society form the foundation for building a just and harmonious world, where honesty, justice, compassion, respect for human dignity, and responsibility toward society and nature are recognized. These values must serve as a guide for all social and legal institutions, fostering the creation of a sustainable and ethically balanced environment.
No ideology, religion, political theory, or cultural practice can be placed above universal moral principles. All laws and governmental decisions must be based on the highest moral standards, ensuring equality, justice, and respect for all, regardless of nationality, race, gender, religion, cultural background, or social status.
States are obligated to develop and implement educational programs aimed at fostering moral and ethical qualities in citizens, such as honesty, tolerance, responsibility, and respect for human rights. These principles must be integrated at all levels of education, with a focus on developing critical thinking and the ability to approach moral issues consciously.
Respect for moral and ethical values implies the recognition and protection of cultural and spiritual diversity. States are obligated to create conditions for dialogue and mutual understanding between different cultures and traditions, promoting respect for every society, regardless of its characteristics.
States are obligated to take effective measures to prevent the spread of ideologies, practices, and actions that undermine the moral foundations of society. This includes combating the propaganda of violence, hatred, discrimination, and all forms of exploitation.
All public institutions, including government bodies, educational establishments, and media, must adhere to ethical principles in their activities. This includes transparency, accountability, and respect for human rights within their operations.
States are obligated to cooperate at the international level to promote universal moral values, exchange best practices in ethical education, and strengthen moral standards on a global scale.
Individuals, organizations, and states that violate moral and ethical norms must bear legal and social responsibility. States are obligated to create effective mechanisms for protecting moral values, including systems for monitoring compliance with ethical standards and supporting victims of moral violations.
Every person has the right to moral freedom of choice, provided that their actions do not infringe on the rights and freedoms of others. States are obligated to protect this right, creating conditions for the free expression of moral beliefs, ensuring that societal morals will not be imposed forcibly.
Civil society plays an essential role in protecting and promoting moral and ethical values. States are obligated to support initiatives aimed at strengthening moral culture and ethical consciousness in society, ensuring that every citizen is free to form and express their beliefs within the framework of universal human values.

Article 45. Guarantees of the Rights of Future Generations

Rights and freedoms extend not only to the present but also to future generations. Humanity bears collective responsibility for preserving the planet, its resources, and cultural heritage, ensuring the conditions for a dignified life for future generations.
Decisions made today must take into account their long-term consequences for ecosystems, society, and cultural heritage. Priority must be given to sustainable development, minimizing risks, and preventing ecological and social crises.
States, organizations, and individuals are obligated to prevent pollution, depletion of natural resources, loss of biodiversity, and climate change. The use of natural resources must be rational, considering the need to conserve them for future generations.
Every generation is obligated to protect, develop, and pass on cultural, scientific, and spiritual achievements to ensure the continuity of knowledge and traditions that contribute to the development of human civilization.
All political, economic, and social strategies must be developed with consideration of long-term perspectives. Potential risks and consequences of decisions affecting the rights and well-being of future generations must be taken into account.
In conditions of scientific or environmental uncertainty, precautionary measures should be prioritized to prevent potential harm to the environment and the health of future generations.
Scientific and technological developments must consider potential risks to future generations. Priority should be given to projects that promote sustainable development, health conservation, and improvement in quality of life.
States are obligated to provide access to quality education, fostering in youth an awareness of the importance of environmental protection, social responsibility, and global solidarity.
States are obligated to cooperate at the international level to protect the interests of future generations, exchange advanced technologies, and implement joint initiatives aimed at sustainable development and the preservation of the planet.
Individuals, organizations, and states that make decisions that harm the environment, cultural heritage, or societal sustainability shall bear legal and moral responsibility. Legal protection mechanisms must consider the rights and interests of future generations.

Article 46. Responsibility for the Long-Term Consequences of Decisions

Every political, economic, social, or ecological decision must be made with consideration of its long-term consequences for humanity, the environment, and future generations. The principle of sustainability requires all governing entities to consider intergenerational justice and minimize negative risks.
Leaders, organizations, and states are obligated to consider the moral and ethical consequences of their decisions. The principles of ethical leadership prioritize universal human values, social justice, and environmental protection.
In conditions of scientific, technological, or ecological uncertainty, decisions should be made with utmost caution. States are obligated to prevent actions that could cause irreversible harm to society, nature, or cultural heritage.
All projects, programs, and legislative initiatives must be accompanied by a comprehensive assessment of their short-term and long-term impacts on society, the economy, the environment, human health, and cultural values. This assessment must include the participation of independent experts and civil society representatives.
States and international organizations are obligated to develop and implement sustainable development strategies aimed at preventing long-term risks, such as climate change, loss of biodiversity, and depletion of natural resources. These strategies must include mechanisms for adaptation and mitigation of impacts on the most vulnerable populations.
Decisions with potential long-term consequences must be made transparently and based on broad public discussion. Government bodies are obligated to provide access to information about risks and expected outcomes, as well as mechanisms for monitoring and feedback.
States are obligated to cooperate at the international level to prevent global threats, exchange best practices, and coordinate actions. Violations of international environmental standards or neglect of global sustainability should be treated as violations of international law, with corresponding legal accountability.
Every person has the right to participate in decision-making that affects the future of society and the environment. This includes the right to access information, participate in public consultations, and engage in legal protection mechanisms, including the possibility of appeal to international bodies.
Individuals, organizations, and states whose actions or inactions have caused significant negative consequences for future generations shall bear legal, moral, and financial responsibility. This includes obligations to remediate damage, compensate victims, and restore disrupted ecosystems and communities.
States are obligated to support research, innovation, and technology development aimed at ensuring sustainable development. Priority should be given to projects that reduce the negative environmental impact and strengthen social and economic sustainability in the long term.

Article 47. Conditions for the Limitation of Rights and Freedoms

Human rights and freedoms are inalienable and protected by law. Their limitations are permissible only in exceptional cases, when necessary for the protection of the public interest, the rights and freedoms of others, public safety, health, morality, or in emergency situations.
Any limitations of rights and freedoms must be clearly defined by law, in accordance with the principles of legal certainty, and must not permit arbitrary decisions by government authorities.
Limitations must be proportional to the objectives to be achieved and applied only to the extent necessary to achieve legitimate goals. Any limitation must be minimal to achieve these goals.
Limitations on rights and freedoms may be imposed only for a limited period of time and are subject to regular review to assess the need for their continuation.
Limitations must not be applied on a discriminatory basis, such as race, gender, language, religion, political views, national or social origin, property status, birth, or any other status.
In emergency situations, such as armed conflict, natural disasters, or public crises, limitations on rights and freedoms may only occur in strict compliance with international standards and are subject to oversight by independent bodies.
Even in emergency situations, the following rights cannot be limited: the right to life, the prohibition of torture and inhuman treatment, freedom from slavery and forced labor, the right to legal assistance, and recognition of legal capacity.
Any limitation of rights and freedoms must be subject to judicial review. Individuals whose rights have been limited have the right to appeal such decisions to an independent and impartial court.
Limitations on rights and freedoms must not contradict the state's international human rights obligations. International treaties and standards take precedence in the event of conflicts with national legislation.
States are obligated to inform the population in a timely manner about the imposition of limitations on rights and freedoms, the grounds for their application, and the duration of these limitations, ensuring transparency and accountability in their actions.

Article 48. Protection of Rights in Emergency Situations

Human rights and freedoms are preserved even in emergency situations. Restrictions on rights are permissible only in strictly defined legal cases and to the extent necessary to ensure public safety, order, and the protection of the rights of others.
The declaration of an emergency must be based on constitutional norms and international legal standards. Any restrictions on rights and freedoms must be temporary, necessary, and proportional to the level of threat.
Emergency measures cannot be used to justify arbitrary actions by authorities, the suppression of peaceful protests, restrictions on freedom of expression, or violations of the rights of minorities.
Even in emergency situations, the following rights cannot be limited: the right to life, freedom from torture, inhuman or degrading treatment, freedom from slavery, the right to judicial protection, and legal capacity.
The introduction and implementation of emergency measures are subject to oversight by independent bodies, including the judiciary and international human rights organizations. Individuals responsible for abusing emergency powers are legally accountable.
States are obligated to take additional measures to protect vulnerable groups in emergency situations, including children, the elderly, persons with disabilities, migrants, and refugees.
States are obligated to provide citizens with access to reliable and timely information about the nature of the emergency, its consequences, and the measures being implemented.
In emergency situations, states are obligated to comply with international treaties in the field of human rights. Restrictions on rights must not contradict international obligations and standards.
Every person has the right to appeal restrictions on their rights imposed in emergency situations before independent judicial bodies.
All emergency measures must be terminated immediately after the circumstances that caused them have been resolved. The extension of the state of emergency is permissible only in exceptional cases and when there are valid reasons, supported by independent analysis.

Article 49. Responsibility for Violations of Rights and Freedoms

The protection of human rights and freedoms is accompanied by established legal, moral, and social responsibility for their violation. Violating rights and freedoms is unacceptable and results in consequences in accordance with international standards and legal norms.
Every individual is personally responsible for their actions or inactions that lead to the violation of the rights and freedoms of others. This includes responsibility both on an individual level and within professional or public activities.
States are responsible for violations of rights and freedoms within their territory. This includes the obligation to prevent violations, protect victims, and ensure access to justice for all citizens.
International organizations are obligated to respect human rights and freedoms in their activities. Violations of rights by international structures are subject to international legal responsibility.
Individuals and organizations whose rights have been violated have the right to fair compensation and the restoration of their rights. This may include financial compensation, an official apology, reinstatement at work, or other measures aimed at remedying the consequences of the violation.
Severe violations of human rights, such as genocide, crimes against humanity, war crimes, and acts of terrorism, are subject to criminal prosecution under national and international judicial bodies.
In addition to criminal liability, violations of rights and freedoms may be subject to administrative and civil measures, including fines, restrictions on professional activities, and other sanctions.
Individuals holding governmental or public positions bear enhanced responsibility for upholding rights and freedoms. Abuse of office to violate citizens' rights is subject to strict legal penalties.
States are obligated to cooperate in the investigation and prosecution of human rights violations, exchange information, and assist international judicial bodies in achieving justice.
No one may be exempted from responsibility for violating rights and freedoms, regardless of their status, position, or office held. Responsibility is based on the law and cannot be limited by time or other circumstances.

Article 50. Duties of Citizens and Individuals

Every person bears personal and collective responsibility for respecting, observing, and protecting the rights and freedoms of others. The pursuit of personal interests must not be achieved at the expense of violating the rights of others. Any behavior that threatens public order, the rights, and safety of others is subject to legal responsibility.
Every citizen is obligated to obey laws established in accordance with the Constitution and to contribute to maintaining law and order, social justice, and harmony in society. This includes the duty to honestly fulfill civil, tax, labor, and other legal obligations.
Every person is obligated to respect and uphold the moral and ethical principles that ensure peaceful coexistence, social cohesion, and inclusiveness of society. This includes adhering to non-violence, rejecting discrimination, promoting social responsibility, and respecting cultural diversity.
Every citizen bears responsibility for the preservation of the environment, rational use of natural resources, and prevention of ecological harm. The duty of ecological responsibility includes compliance with environmental protection standards, supporting sustainable consumption, and participating in initiatives to protect ecosystems.
Every person is obligated to contribute to the development of society and support its cultural, economic, and social progress. This includes active participation in elections, local governance, public initiatives, education, and the formation of legal culture.
Every citizen is obligated to strengthen equality and social justice, eliminate discrimination, support the protection of vulnerable groups, uphold social protection mechanisms, and act in the public interest.
Every person is obligated to respect public institutions and contribute to their effective functioning. This includes respecting the judiciary, participating in the maintenance of lawfulness, executing judicial decisions, and fulfilling obligations to society.
Every citizen is obligated to respect intellectual property, scientific achievements, and cultural heritage. This includes compliance with copyright laws, supporting education and scientific research, and protecting traditional knowledge and values.
Every person is obligated to contribute to the peaceful resolution of disputes, avoid violence, extremism, and destructive activities. This includes the duty to resolve conflicts through lawful methods, maintain dialogue, and respect international norms of peaceful coexistence.
Every citizen is obligated to respect the principles of the rule of law, support transparency and accountability in government, and contribute to the fight against corruption, abuse of power, and illegal economic activities.
Every person bears personal responsibility for their development, education, and skills enhancement in order to contribute to the sustainable development of society. The state is obligated to create conditions for acquiring knowledge, but the responsibility for learning and applying it rests with each citizen.
Individuals who evade their civic duties, contribute to social inequality, violate principles of public solidarity, or knowingly harm society are legally and socially responsible according to the law.

Article 51. Duties of the State in the Field of Human Rights

The state bears primary responsibility for the protection, fulfillment, and promotion of human rights and freedoms within its jurisdiction. It is obligated to respect, protect, and implement the rights of every individual guaranteed by the Constitution, international law, and principles of social justice.
The state is obligated to develop, implement, and maintain a legislative framework that ensures the protection of human rights, elimination of all forms of discrimination and inequality, and ensures equal opportunities for all citizens.
The state is obligated to provide access to independent, impartial, and effective justice, create conditions for the protection of citizens' rights, and monitor the activities of law enforcement and judicial bodies to prevent abuse.
The state is obligated to actively prevent any form of exploitation, violence, abuse, torture, and discrimination. It is responsible for developing mechanisms to protect victims of human rights violations and to hold perpetrators accountable.
The state is obligated to ensure that every person has access to basic public goods, such as education, healthcare, housing, social security, environmental safety, and economic stability.
The state is obligated to ensure equal access to information for citizens, including transparency in governance, access to official documents, and protection of freedom of speech. Restrictions may only be applied in exceptional cases, as established by law, and should not violate the principles of openness and accountability in government.
The state is obligated to implement a policy of social justice aimed at eliminating poverty, reducing social inequality, and supporting vulnerable groups. It must develop assistance programs and establish effective social protection mechanisms.
The state is obligated to implement and maintain systems of civic participation, providing citizens with opportunities to engage in governance processes and to participate in decision-making that affects their lives and society as a whole.
The state is obligated to protect and support cultural diversity, national heritage, and the languages of peoples. It must take measures to preserve, develop, and disseminate cultural values while respecting the traditions of all ethnic and social groups.
The state bears responsibility for adhering to international human rights obligations, actively participating in international initiatives for their protection, implementing decisions of international courts and human rights organizations, and preventing any violations of international law.
The state is obligated to fight corruption, abuse of power, and economic crimes, ensuring transparency in state institutions, a system of public oversight, and effective mechanisms to prevent violations.
The state is responsible for protecting the environment and implementing sustainable development policies, including the protection of natural resources, environmental education, and monitoring economic activities that may harm ecosystems and populations.
Government bodies and officials who fail to fulfill their duties in the field of human rights protection are subject to legal responsibility, including criminal prosecution and disqualification from holding public office.
The state is obligated to inform citizens about their rights, protection mechanisms, and procedures for seeking assistance in a timely manner. Accessible and clear forms of legal education, training, and public consultation must be developed.
The state is obligated to consider the opinion of civil society, independent expert communities, and human rights organizations when developing and implementing policies in the field of human rights protection.
The state is obligated to develop and implement long-term strategies aimed at strengthening human rights, preventing crises, and creating a just, sustainable, and democratic governance system.
The state is obligated to protect the rights of citizens not only within its territory but also provide diplomatic and consular protection to citizens abroad, advocating for their rights at the international level.
The state bears legal, political, and moral responsibility for human rights violations and is obligated to restore justice and compensate the victims of violations.

Article 52. Duties of Individuals in the Protection of Rights and Freedoms

Every person bears personal responsibility for respecting, observing, and protecting the rights and freedoms of others. They must recognize the equality of all before the law, adhere to principles of justice, and contribute to building a society based on humanism and social welfare.
Every person is obligated not to violate the rights and freedoms of others through their actions or omissions. This includes respect for life, dignity, safety, privacy, property, and the opinions of others.
Every person is obligated to actively participate in the protection of human rights, preventing violations and contributing to the elimination of injustice, discrimination, oppression, and other forms of human rights abuses.
Every person is obligated to oppose any manifestations of violence, intolerance, exploitation, and oppression. This includes condemning and preventing racism, sexism, ethnic and religious hatred, social injustice, and other forms of discrimination.
Every person is obligated to obey laws designed to protect rights and freedoms and to assist in their enforcement in society. Ignorance of the law does not exempt one from responsibility for its violation.
Every person is obligated to protect the environment and support sustainable development. This includes mindful consumption, respect for natural resources, preventing pollution, and contributing to ecological initiatives.
Every person is obligated to respect the principles of social justice, including helping vulnerable groups, demonstrating civic solidarity, and contributing to the welfare of society.
Every person is obligated to promote legal literacy, participate in educational and informational initiatives aimed at raising awareness of the rights and duties of citizens.
Every person is obligated to follow principles of honesty, integrity, humanity, and respect for others in both public and personal relationships.
Every person is obligated to prevent and expose instances of corruption, abuse of power, unjust distribution of resources, and other social issues affecting rights and freedoms.
Every person is obligated to participate in public life, vote in elections, support democratic processes, and contribute to the formation of a just state.
Every person, having sufficient knowledge and resources, is obligated to pass on their knowledge and experience to future generations, teaching them to respect rights, freedoms, humanism, and responsibility.
Every person is obligated to comply with contractual obligations, legal agreements, and principles of accountability to society, the state, and future generations.
Every person who discovers a violation of rights or a threat to human rights is obligated to take any possible legal measures to protect those rights, including addressing human rights organizations, government bodies, and international institutions.
Every person is obligated to bear legal and social responsibility for their actions if they result in the violation of the rights and freedoms of others.

Article 53. Duties of Government Bodies and Institutions

Government bodies and institutions are obligated to ensure the protection, observance, and promotion of human rights and freedoms throughout their jurisdiction, prevent violations of these rights, and guarantee equality before the law for all individuals.
Government bodies are obligated to ensure the rule of law, independence, and impartiality of the judiciary, as well as provide every person with access to justice. They must prevent arbitrary application of the law, protect citizens from abuse of power, and ensure accountability of public officials.
Government institutions are obligated to develop and implement legislative and administrative measures aimed at eliminating discrimination, inequality, and injustice, creating conditions for equal access to opportunities, resources, and rights for all citizens.
Government bodies are obligated to ensure transparency in their activities, guarantee the right of citizens to receive accurate information about government decisions and processes, and provide mechanisms for civil oversight of government actions.

Article 57. Global System of Economic Democracy

Economic democracy is the foundation of global governance, ensuring the fair distribution of resources, the elimination of economic inequality, and the creation of sustainable economic structures focused on the welfare of society. Economic policy is aimed at supporting cooperative forms of ownership, local production, small and medium-sized businesses, and preventing the monopolization of key industries. Mechanisms are created to protect against the concentration of capital in the hands of a few individuals, excluding the possibility of exploiting economic systems for private interests.
The financial system is based on the principles of ethical economics, excluding speculative operations, the creation of artificial financial crises, excessive interest burdens, and any forms of financial manipulation. Government investments and subsidies are directed to priority sectors that ensure the public good: healthcare, education, science, the environment, social security, infrastructure, and innovation.

Every person has the right to fair working conditions, just pay, social protection, and participation in the management of their working communities. Exploitation, forced labor, child labor, and discrimination in the workplace are strictly prohibited.
Economic policy encourages the development of environmentally friendly technologies, the green economy, and innovative solutions aimed at reducing the impact of economic activities on the environment. Government support is provided to enterprises that operate in accordance with the principles of sustainable development.
One of the priorities of global economic policy is the elimination of poverty, closing the gap between developed and developing regions, and ensuring economic justice. International mechanisms are created to support economically disadvantaged regions, fostering equal living standards worldwide.
International trade and economic cooperation are regulated based on the principles of fairness, mutually beneficial exchange, and preventing unequal trade relations that lead to exploitation and worsen the situation of economically weaker countries.
The economic system must be open and accountable to citizens. Mechanisms for democratic control, public consultations, and regular audits of the economic activities of government and transnational entities are established.
The implementation of the principles of economic democracy is an essential condition for the functioning of the World Government.
